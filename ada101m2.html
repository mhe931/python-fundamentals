Lecture 1: Data Architecture Concepts (DA)

DA as the "Blueprint and Governance" for organizational data systems.

  • Defines how data is structured, stored, and flows.
  • Ensures alignment with business goals and compliance.
Key Insight: DA isn't just tech—it's the strategic foundation preventing data chaos.
Start here: DA sets the rules of the road for all data initiatives. Review this before diving into roles.

Comparing Roles in Data Teams

RoleFocusKey Responsibilities
DA (Architect)StrategicBlueprint design, Governance policies
DE (Engineer)OperationalInfrastructure build, ETL/ELT pipelines
DS (Scientist)AnalyticalData consumption, Model insights & ML

Contrast: DA focuses on "why" and "how overall," DE on "build it," DS on "what can we learn?" Collaboration is key—silos lead to failures.

Exam tip: Know the handoffs—e.g., DA defines schemas that DE implements for DS to query.

Data Models & Philosophy

  • Models Progression:
    • Conceptual: High-level entities.
    • Logical: Attributes & keys.
    • Physical: Data types & storage paths.
  • Philosophy: Form Follows Function
    • Design matches business needs.
    • Example: Fraud detection → Streaming architecture for real-time processing.
Remember: Start conceptual, refine to physical. Philosophy ensures no over-engineering—tailor to use case.

Lecture 2: Foundational Principles

Core Principles: Trust, Scale, Modularity

  • Trust: Reliable, governed data.
  • Scale: Handles growth without breaking.
  • Modularity: Interchangeable components.
Fun Fact: These principles trace back to 1980s enterprise architecture—timeless!
These are the "why" behind DA decisions. Link them to real-world scalability pains in exams.

Trust & Ownership

  • Single Source of Truth (SSOT): One master system per data element—avoids duplication & inconsistencies.
  • Data as a Product: Treat data like software: Own it, manage SLAs, measure utility (e.g., freshness, accuracy).

Benefits: Reduces errors by 50%+ in large orgs.

SSOT example: Customer data in CRM only—reference elsewhere. Data Product: Assign owners like product managers.

Scale & Structure

  • Scalability: Use Loose Coupling via Broker Pattern / Pub-Sub to isolate systems (e.g., Kafka prevents DB overload).
  • Frameworks:
    FrameworkPurpose
    TOGAFProcess/Roadmap for EA evolution
    ZachmanTaxonomy/Checklist for "What, How, Where"
Loose coupling: Systems communicate asynchronously. TOGAF for planning, Zachman for inventory—complementary.

Lecture 3: Data Warehousing (DWH)

DWH: The home for structured data and OLAP analytics.

  • Centralized repository for reporting & BI.
  • Optimized for queries, not transactions.
DWH is "batch-oriented" vs. OLTP's real-time. Key for historical analysis.

DWH vs. Data Lake Comparison

AspectDWHData Lake
Data TypeStructured OnlyAll Types (Structured, Semi, Unstructured)
ProcessETL (Extract-Transform-Load)ELT (Extract-Load-Transform)
SchemaSchema-on-Write (Rigid)Schema-on-Read (Flexible)

ETL Note: Staging Area is where "T" (Transform) occurs—temporary zone for cleaning before loading to DWH.

Choose DWH for known queries; Lake for exploration. ETL staging prevents dirty data from hitting production.

DWH Model & OLAP Operations

  • Model: Star Schema
    • Central Fact Table (metrics).
    • Surrounding Dimension Tables (context, e.g., time, product).
  • OLAP Operations:
    • Roll-Up (↑): Aggregate to less detail (e.g., daily → monthly).
    • Drill-Down (↓): Expand to more detail (e.g., monthly → daily).
    • Slice/Dice: Filter & pivot dimensions.
Fun Fact: Star Schema looks like a star—simple but powerful for fast queries!
Practice: Fact = sales amount; Dims = customer, date. OLAP enables "what-if" analysis.

Lecture 4: Data Lakes and Modern DW

Covering Data Lakes (variety of data), and evolution to Modern DW.

  • Lakes store raw data cheaply.
  • Modern DW blends lake flexibility with warehouse reliability.
Lakes handle "big data" volume/variety; modern trends unify approaches.

Design & Risks

  • Design: Bottom-Up Approach
    • Ingest raw data first.
    • Analyze needs.
    • Then structure (e.g., zones).
  • Risk: Data Swamp
    • Cause: Lack of metadata & governance.
    • Result: Unusable "garbage dump."
Key Insight: Govern early—metadata catalogs prevent swamps.
Bottom-up vs. top-down: Start flexible, add structure. Exam: Link swamp to poor DA.

Solutions to Lake Challenges

  • Solution 1: Layered Architecture
    • Raw (Bronze): Ingested as-is.
    • Cleaned (Silver): Validated & enriched.
    • Curated (Gold): Business-ready, aggregated.
  • Solution 2: Data Lakehouse
    • Convergence: Adds ACID transactions & schema enforcement to Lakes.
    • Tools: Delta Lake, Iceberg.
Layers promote progressive refinement. Lakehouse = best of both worlds—reliable like DWH, scalable like Lake.
